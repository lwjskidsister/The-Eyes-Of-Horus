About The Eyes of Horus

Imagine stepping back in time to a land where history and legend intertwine, where majestic monuments and intricate artistry tell the tales of a civilization that shaped the course of human development. Welcome to Eye of Horus, a history voyage that will transport you to the wonders of ancient Egypt, one of the greatest civilizations on Earth. As we embark on this journey, prepare to be immersed in the marvels of a world where every artifact and monument serves as a window into a remarkable past, inviting you to witness the legacy of a civilization that continues to fascinate and inspire.

Most importantly: The Rosetta Stone

The Rosetta Stone was created in 196 BCE during the reign of Ptolemy V. It contains a decree issued by priests to honor the young king, written in Greek, Demotic, and hieroglyphs so it could be understood by different groups. It was discovered by French soldiers in Egypt in 1799. The reason the “Rosetta Stone” is incredibly important because it allowed scholars to finally decipher ancient Egyptian hieroglyphs, a language that had been lost for centuries. After Napoleon’s defeat, the British took the stone in 1801, and it has been in the British Museum since 1802. Its discovery and translation opened the door to understanding ancient Egypt's history, culture, and written language. Learn more and see more here

Secondly: The Bust of Nefertiti

The Bust of Nefertiti was crafted around 1345 BCE by the sculptor Thutmose in his workshop in Amarna. It is a limestone sculpture of Queen Nefertiti, wife of Pharaoh Akhenaten, and is known for its stunning craftsmanship and beauty. It was likely created as a model to showcase Queen Nefertiti’s beauty and royal stature. Found in Thutmose's workshop, it has become one of the most iconic representations of ancient Egyptian art. The Bust of Nefertiti was discovered in 1912 by a German archaeological team led by Ludwig Borchardt in Amarna, Egypt. The bust was taken to Germany, where it remains on display at the Neues Museum in Berlin. Egypt has repeatedly requested its return, but Germany has refused. Learn more and see more here

At third place: The Dendera Zodiac

The Dendera Zodiac was made around 50 BCE as part of the Temple of Hathor’s ceiling in Dendera, Egypt. It’s an elaborate relief showing constellations and celestial events, reflecting ancient Egyptian knowledge of the stars. The zodiac is historically important because it provides insights into the Egyptians' understanding of astronomy and their religious beliefs linked to the stars. It was discovered by French archaeologist Claude Sicard in 1820 during Napoleon’s campaign in Egypt.The zodiac was taken to France and is now housed in the Louvre Museum in Paris. At fourth place: The Luxor Obelisks

The Luxor Obelisks were originally erected in front of the Luxor Temple in Egypt around 1300 BCE by Pharaoh Ramesses II. They are tall, four-sided monuments covered in hieroglyphs that celebrate the pharaoh’s reign. Historically, they are significant as an example of ancient Egyptian monumental art and engineering. The Luxor Obelisks were standing at the Luxor Temple when Muhammad Ali Pasha, in 1833, gifted one of them to France and it was moved to Paris. It now stands in the Place de la Concorde. Egypt has requested its return, but the obelisk remains in France. Learn more and see more here

At fifth place: The Sarcophagus of Pharaoh Seti I

The Sarcophagus of Pharaoh Seti I is a large alabaster coffin used to contain the mummified remains of Pharaoh Seti I. It was made around 1300 BCE and is renowned for its elaborate carvings depicting funerary texts, religious symbols, and gods, which were intended to protect the pharaoh in the afterlife. British archaeologist Giovanni Belzoni discovered the sarcophagus in 1821 in the Valley of the Kings, where it was originally entombed. It was subsequently transported to England and is now displayed at Sir John Soane’s Museum in London. Egypt has formally requested the return of the sarcophagus, emphasizing its importance to their cultural heritage, but it remains in the UK to this day. Learn more and see more here

Last but not least: The Sphinx of Tanis

The Sphinx of Tanis is a large granite statue with the head of a Pharaoh and a lion's body, created around 26 BCE. It was made to symbolize the Pharaoh’s power and divine protection, embodying the fusion of royal and divine attributes that was central to ancient Egyptian beliefs. The sphinx was discovered in Tanis (modern-day San el-Hagar) in Egypt in 1825 by French Egyptologist Jean-François Champollion. It was later taken to France and is now housed in the Louvre Museum in Paris. Egypt has requested its return, highlighting its importance to their cultural heritage, but it remains in France.
